Significant values

In statistics, an outlier is a data point that differs significantly from the others present in a sample. Often, outliers can alert the results of a study. However, in the environment where PATHFINDER works, outliers usually indicate outstanding and outstanding characteristics in the analyzed data set. That is why your reference is used as significant values . They are, so to speak (and without forgetting that the atypical they have a high degree of subjectivity), the elements of a group that "go out of norm" and, therefore, attract attention.

The most widespread mathematical method to detect outliers is known as Tukey tes , and it uses the difference between the first and the third quartile, (usually known as interquartile range or IQR). First of all, to calculate these elements you must to find the median of the sample, that is, the value of the variable located in the central position of a set of ordered data. From there, the quartiles, which are the three values ​​that divide aforementioned set into four equal size parts; that is where 25% of the elements begins, the first quartile is located, the median is 50% and the third quartile is 75%. Two categories of outliers are usually considered:

  • Mild (1.5 * IQR): If the value is 1.5 times the distance of the interquartile range from one of those quartiles.
  • Extreme (3 * IQR): If the value is 3 times the distance of the interquartile range from one of those quartiles.

Atypical values, therefore, are those located "below" or "above" normality. PATHFINDER, for operational reasons, detects outliers of mild category (1.5 * IQR).
